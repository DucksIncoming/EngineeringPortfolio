Pallet Workbench

Background

While the majority of my projects are heavily design-focused, typically with a mechanism designed to accomplish a unique purpose, this project instead was instead intended to replicate a table design by using discarded wooden shipping pallets. I contacted a few businesses local to our area which regularly received shipments, and they were happy to let me take and use what otherwise would have ended up in a landfill. I spent some time sorting through several and selected two which were dry, heat treated, and in good condition.

Material Preparation

To prepare the pallet wood for processing, I first ripped them down using a pry bar and hammer. I removed all the nails and separated out the several boards. I then laid out the pieces to plan the layout to form the table.

I set aside the highest quality slats to make up the surface of the table, and sorted them by their size so I could plan the table dimensions around what I had available. Next, I ripped apart the wooden support beams and selected the highest quality and most similar ones to later make up the table legs.

These pieces were still not ready to be assembled, so I began ripping each of the boards down to a specified length using a circular saw. These cut down pieces were then quickly sanded using 80-grit sandpaper and an orbital sander, preparing them for working.

Assembly

With the surface boards prepared, I began assembling them using wood glue and a number of clamps. I conducted this assembly in stages as I did not have any clamps large enough for the entire table surface at once. Once this was dry, I put a few screws through the table perpendicular to the direction of the panels for added stability.

Once the tabletop was prepared, I went back over it using my orbital sander and 80-grit sandpaper, removing the bulk material to make the sides parallel and smooth. After this, I moved up to 120-grit and 220-grit, which left an extremely smooth surface, with only a few voids in the material. I used some off-the-shelf wood filler to stabilize these larger voids, then I went over the entire surface with a wood stain and finish. I left this to dry before completing the other sides, then moved on to the legs.

The legs were formed from the supporting beams underneath the original pallet, as their shape was already well-suited to this application and it allowed me to make use of the remaining material from my scrap wood. I cut these pieces down to a roughly equal size and subjected them to the same sanding routine used for the table surface. I also treated these legs with the same finish, and installed a screw into the bottom of each leg so the height could be adjusted.

Results

The table turned out fantastic and I was able to limit my material usage only to the freely-sourced pallet wood I was able to obtain from local businesses. This project gave me a great deal of hands-on experience with power tools and material preparation, and it definitely helped to develop my understanding of how a project must be planned around the fabrication equipment available, and I was extremely proud of the result of this project. The table is still standing to this day and I still use it as a workbench and storage location for my tools.
