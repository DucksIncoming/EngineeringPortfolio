CheapliSafe: Home Security System

Background

In early 2025, my apartment was broken into and robbed. I was not home at the time, thankfully, but my roommates unfortunately were, and they were unfortunately caught in the middle of it.

Both of them made it out with only minor injuries in the end, but the entire experience definitely shook away any feelings of security or safety I had while in that building. I had exactly one goal for the rest of that month: make sure that can't happen again.

Solution #1: Remote Peephole-Camera

When our apartment was robbed, the invaders used our neighbor as a decoy to feign entry and get my roommate to open the door, since he could only see a small area outside our door through the peephole. Having so little vision of our front hallway poses a pretty significant security vulnerability, so the first component for this project sought to provide a better field of view.

I had a PCB camera laying around from a previous project, and it seemed like a perfect component to use for the hallway camera. I used the dimensions of this camera as the basis for the SolidWorks design for the camera mount. To get a full view of the hallway, this camera would ideally be able to be adjusted remotely. For this design, I planned for two axes of rotation, allowing the user to change the yaw and pitch of the camera so any part of the hallway can be in view. To accomplish this, I used two servo motors. I used a larger MG995 servo for the base assembly, since this pivot would need to withstand much higher loads, and used a small SG90 servo for the pitch control.

This design went through two iterations, with the first prototype being 3D-printed on a Prusa Mk4 printer using PLA. This model was to ensure the part scale was accurate and to test mounting locations in the hallway so I could better plan the mounting plate design. Following this initial prototype, I recreated the assembly with the necessary tolerances to allow the moving parts to operate. I also designed several 3D printed screw mounts to avoid having to purchase any additional hardware.

To ensure the clearance was sufficient for the mechanism to operate, I ran a SolidWorks Motion Study to determine the limiting positions. Both the yaw and pitch range were well within what was expected, and the motion was plenty sufficient for what would cover the hallway. With the final design completed, I used PrusaSlicer to add supports and generate the g-code, then 3D printed the parts again using PLA on a Prusa Mk4.

Remote Peephole-Camera: Performance

To operate the camera, I created a Raspberry Pi program designed to interface with a 7" touchscreen meant for use with RasPis. I then created a small frame to hold the display and mounted it near the door to show a constant video feed from the camera. I also included a WiFi card which allows for the camera to be controlled over Wi-Fi. I added a toggle switch to the Raspberry Pi circuit to enable or disable the wireless feature of the camera, because leaving a Wi-Fi enabled security camera running 24/7 could pose some security risks.

The camera operated exactly as intended, and the controls to change the camera view are very intuitive and easy to understand. Not only that, but this new solution gives us a view of our entire hallway entrance, allowing us to prepare for whoever may be at the door before we answer. While this won't keep us physically protected in the case of an armed intruder, it will give us an early warning as to their presence and potentially save us from having to deal with the situation entirely.

Solution #2: Cheaplisafe, an All-In DIY Home Security System

Along with the security camera build detailed above, this project also sought to create a number of security devices that could be combined together to form one cohesive security system. The most important components for this build are:

  • A Main Unit capable of sending + receiving commands
  • A keypad-enabled Alarm System
  • Door/Window Entry sensors
  • A Glass Break Sensor
  • A hallway Motion Sensor
  • A Panic Button to automatically trigger the alarm

The specific components to be built were selected to model the products offered by SimpliSafe®, as these systems encompass a wide variety of security products and offer an opportunity for direct cost comparison between the commercial system and my DIY solution.

The table above shows the price of a solution from SimpliSafe, coming to a total of $359.93 for a relatively basic security system. This price is extremely high, however the "Cheaplisafe" solution was able to reduce this cost by $290.88 while maintaining the same functionality. The greatest cost savings came from the central unit and alarm systems.

These components were successful in providing protection and alerting the user to disturbances to their secure perimeter. Additionally, through the design of the central unit, this system can theoretically accomodate an unlimited number of additional components, so long as they are able to transmit an alarm signal to the central unit. This piezo buzzer alarm can be replaced with a more effective component, such as one which sends automated texts to your phone in the case of a break-in.
